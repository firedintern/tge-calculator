## Design Context

### Users
Pre-investment crypto analysts doing serious due diligence. Not degens panic-buying — people who want to stress-test tokenomics before committing capital. They're methodical, experienced enough to be skeptical, and come here for clarity, not reassurance. Usage context: desktop-first, focused research sessions.

### Brand Personality
**Sharp. Ruthless. Professional.**

The interface should feel like a Bloomberg terminal crossed with a forensic audit report. It doesn't flatter projects — it dissects them. No softening language, no friendly gradients, no "you got this!" energy. Data is the hero. The result should feel like a verdict, not a suggestion.

Emotional goal: a gut punch of reality. When the result is HIGH pressure, the user should feel it viscerally. When it's LOW, they should feel genuinely reassured — not because the UI is comforting, but because the numbers earned it.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Theme**: Dark. Analysts work in dark environments, at night, with multiple monitors. Dark mode is not a style choice here — it's the correct choice for the audience and context.
- **Visual tone**: Editorial/financial. Think: The Financial Times data desk, a forensic risk report, a hedge fund terminal. NOT: crypto exchange UI, SaaS dashboard, neon DeFi aesthetic.
- **Anti-references**: No purple gradients. No generic SaaS card layouts. No glowing neon accents. No CoinMarketCap-style busyness. No Comic Sans (was a joke, replacing it).
- **Color**: Deep, near-black background with a warm-tinted neutral (not cold blue-black). A single sharp accent — not purple, not cyan. Consider amber/gold (the color of warning lights and financial data terminals) or a clinical red.
- **Typography**: Something with editorial authority. A condensed or semi-condensed sans for numbers and labels. A distinct display face for the title that reads serious, not playful.

### Design Principles
1. **Data is the hero** — typography, spacing, and layout exist to serve the numbers, not to decorate around them.
2. **Verdicts, not suggestions** — risk ratings should feel absolute and weighty, not softened by rounded corners and pastel badges.
3. **No comfort aesthetics** — avoid anything that makes the interface feel "friendly" or "approachable." This tool is a reality check.
4. **Precision over decoration** — every visual element must earn its place. If it doesn't clarify or emphasize data, it goes.
5. **Professional skepticism** — the UI should feel like it was built by someone who has seen too many projects blow up at TGE and is done being optimistic.
